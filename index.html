The research

This game is built on a body of neuroscience and cognitive psychology research. What follows is a narrative overview of that research — the concepts, findings, and their connections to what you're experiencing in the simulation.

Cognitive Offloading and the Extended Mind

When humans use external tools to reduce the mental work they do internally, cognitive scientists call this cognitive offloading. The concept isn't new — we've always used notebooks, calendars, and calculators. What AI changes is the scale and the intimacy of the offloading. Andy Clark and David Chalmers argued in their 1998 paper The Extended Mind that cognition doesn't stop at the skull — tools we genuinely rely on become part of our cognitive system. If that argument is right, then AI doing your thinking isn't just convenient. It changes who is doing the thinking. The question this raises isn't whether AI is useful. It's: what happens to you when the thinking part of your extended mind is external?

Risko and Gilbert formalised cognitive offloading in 2016 and identified a critical asymmetry: offloading reduces immediate cognitive load — that's the benefit — but habitual offloading reduces the internal rehearsal that builds long-term competency. The short-term gain and the long-term cost operate on different timescales, which is partly why the tradeoff is so hard to perceive in the moment.

Clark & Chalmers (1998). Analysis, 58(1), 7–19 · Risko & Gilbert (2016). Trends in Cognitive Sciences, 20(9), 676–688

What people actually use AI for

The four tasks in this simulation weren't chosen arbitrarily. They map directly onto the most documented patterns of AI use. A 2025 HEPI survey found that 88% of students now use AI for assessment preparation, up from 53% in 2024. The top use cases are explaining concepts (58%), summarising articles and textbooks, and generating research ideas — precisely the tasks that most require active cognitive engagement to produce durable learning. Among higher education faculty and staff, the most common uses are summarising content (74%) and brainstorming on work challenges (71%). In the workplace, 62% of knowledge workers want AI for email drafting, spreadsheet sorting, and meeting-note summarisation, with 35% specifically wanting help with email. This simulation wasn't designed to be representative of all AI use — it was designed to focus on the uses where the cognitive tradeoff is most significant.

HEPI (2025) · EDUCAUSE (2025) · FlexOS/Azumo (2024)

The Neuroscience of Writing

Writing activates a far richer neural network than passive or delegated tasks. Shah et al.'s fMRI research on creative writing found that brainstorming engaged a parieto-frontal-temporal network alongside visual and imaginative processing. Creative writing additionally activated motor and visual brain areas. Most critically, when researchers compared creative writing against simple copying as a control, the most distinctive activation appeared in the hippocampus and temporal poles — regions associated with episodic memory and meaning-making. Copying strips the hippocampal engagement that makes writing cognitively formative. There is also evidence that the brain processes human-authored text differently from machine-generated text, suggesting that AI-generated content produces a neurologically shallower engagement in the reader.

The contrast the simulation shows is grounded in this research: when you write, your hippocampus, temporal poles, motor cortex, Broca's area, and prefrontal cortex are broadly active and interconnected. When AI writes and you read the output, primarily your visual cortex activates. The engagement is narrow, shallow, and not generative.

Shah et al. (2013). Human Brain Mapping, 34(5) · Van der Weel & Van der Meer (2023). Frontiers in Psychology, 14 · Scientific Reports (2019)

Your Brain on ChatGPT

The most direct evidence for what AI writing delegation does to the brain comes from an MIT Media Lab study by Kosmyna et al. (2025). Participants were divided into three groups — LLM-assisted, search engine, and brain-only — each completing essay writing tasks across multiple sessions. EEG revealed significant differences in brain connectivity: brain-only participants exhibited the strongest, most distributed neural networks; search engine users showed moderate engagement; and LLM users displayed the weakest connectivity. Cognitive activity scaled down in direct relation to external tool use. Participants who switched from AI assistance to writing alone showed reduced alpha and beta connectivity — signs of under-engagement — suggesting the brain had already begun adapting downward. This happened within four months. AI-assisted writers were also significantly less likely to recall what they had written and reported less ownership over their work. Independent evaluators found AI-supported essays lacking in individuality and creativity. Notably, participants who wrote independently first and then used AI showed different and more engaged results — the order and habit of use matter.

Kosmyna et al. (2025). arXiv:2506.08872 · Frontiers in Computational Neuroscience (2025)

The Generation Effect and Memory Consolidation

The generation effect is one of the most replicated findings in cognitive psychology: information that learners generate themselves is remembered significantly better than information they simply read or receive. An fMRI study by Rosner et al. found that generating target words activated a broad neural network spanning the prefrontal cortex — specifically the inferior and middle frontal gyri — and posterior regions including the inferior temporal gyrus, parahippocampal gyrus, and ventral posterior parietal cortex. Self-generation lights up a distributed, interconnected network. Reading someone else's output does not produce this pattern. Recall of text has also been shown to improve with self-generated cues — participants who generated keywords from memory showed more complete recall than those who didn't, with effects persisting over retention intervals of up to six weeks. Robert Bjork's concept of desirable difficulties extends this: conditions that slow down or complicate learning in the short term, like having to generate answers rather than read them, produce stronger long-term retention. The short-term inconvenience is the mechanism of the long-term benefit.

Slamecka & Graf (1978). J. Exp. Psych., 4(6) · Rosner et al. (2013). Cortex, 49(8) · McCurdy et al. (2020). Psychonomic Bulletin & Review · Bjork (1994). Metacognition, MIT Press

The Neuroscience of Summarising

Reading an AI summary activates a specific kind of cognitive illusion that is worth understanding precisely. When people read a well-written AI summary, they feel like they understand the content. The neuroscience shows that feeling is largely false. It is easy to mistake "I remember having consumed information" for "I remember that information." Just because information was once held in your brain doesn't mean it's still retrievable — this is called the illusion of competence. With email delegation, you at least know you didn't write it. With AI summaries, you genuinely feel like you learned the content — but the encoding that would make it retrievable simply didn't happen.

Summarising a text yourself is a form of retrieval practice: you force your brain to reconstruct meaning from memory, activating the same prefrontal-hippocampal network the generation effect literature identifies. Reading an AI summary is the opposite — passive re-exposure without reconstruction. fMRI evidence suggests retrieval practice strengthens retention through a dual action on the anterior and posterior hippocampus simultaneously, with encoding and consolidation working together. This dual hippocampal mechanism is precisely what is bypassed when you read someone else's summary instead of constructing your own. There is one important nuance: readers who summarised texts poorly had lower essay scores than readers who simply reread them. Bad self-summarising is worse than passive rereading. This supports the counter-argument that a well-made AI summary might outperform a poorly-made human one — the research doesn't deny this. What it establishes is that a genuinely engaged human summary is cognitively superior to both.

Karpicke & Roediger (2008). Science, 319 · Pastötter & Bäuml (2014). Frontiers in Psychology · Huynh et al. (2024). Contemporary Educational Psychology, 76 · Rowland (2014). Psychological Bulletin, 140(6)

The Neuroscience of Brainstorming and the Default Mode Network

The default mode network — a set of brain regions that are most active during rest, mind-wandering, and imagination — is also the network most suppressed by passive, externally-directed cognitive activity. Creative thinking correlates with unexpected, synchronous activation of the DMN alongside other large-scale networks. A causal link between the DMN and creativity has now been demonstrated through direct cortical stimulation during awake brain surgery, confirming that the network isn't merely correlated with creative thinking but necessary for it. Genuine brainstorming requires a coordinated interaction between two systems: the associative imagination of the DMN and the executive judgment of the frontoparietal network. The DMN generates remote associations; the frontoparietal network evaluates them and selects the useful ones. Beaty et al. (2025) found that dynamic switching between these networks predicts an individual's creative ability across a sample of 2,433 participants.

When AI brainstorms for you, it replaces both processes simultaneously. It doesn't just save you the generative effort — it eliminates the evaluative process that makes your ideas distinctly yours.

Bartoli et al. (2024). Brain, 147(10) · Beaty et al. (2025). Communications Biology · Beaty et al. (2014). Neuropsychologia, 64

The Homogenisation Problem

There is a social dimension to AI brainstorming that individual-level research doesn't capture. While AI-assisted brainstorming produces ideas rated as more creative individually, AI-assisted idea sets consistently show lower diversity. In a toy design study, only 6% of ChatGPT-generated ideas were unique compared to 100% of human ideas. AI-assisted writers produced stories rated as more creative and enjoyable, but these stories were significantly more similar to each other than those written by humans alone. Doshi and Hauser (2024) describe this as a social dilemma: individual gains come at the cost of collective novelty. The diversity gap widens with scale, and enhancing AI's creative diversity through parameter or prompt modifications does not close it. There is an important nuance here: writers with lower creative potential benefitted significantly from AI brainstorming assistance. AI is a genuine equaliser for lower-confidence or lower-ability users. The homogenisation problem is a population-level effect, not a universal individual one.

Meincke, Nave & Terwiesch (2025). Nature Human Behaviour · Doshi & Hauser (2024). Science Advances, 10(28) · Anderson et al. (2024). Creativity and Cognition

Attention, Emotional Regulation, and Impulsivity

The cognitive effects of digital tool use extend beyond the specific tasks being delegated. Gloria Mark's research at UC Irvine documents a dramatic decline in sustained attention: the average time before switching focus on a digital device was 150 seconds in 2004, fell to 75 seconds by 2012, and reached 47 seconds in 2024. Internet addiction has been associated with decreased grey matter in the orbitofrontal cortex — part of the prefrontal cortex — with associated interference in planning and a bias toward immediate rewards. The mere presence of a smartphone reduces basal cognitive performance, even without active use.

Emotional regulation and executive function share the same prefrontal-amygdala circuit. Frequent social media use alters dopamine pathways in ways that compromise decision-making and increase emotional sensitivity. When AI handles cognitively demanding tasks, it removes the productive frustration and effortful engagement that the prefrontal cortex needs to exercise its regulatory function over the amygdala. The key connection: the same prefrontal regions that do your thinking also do your emotional regulation. Consistently offloading thinking to AI may reduce the exercise these regions receive, potentially weakening their regulatory capacity over emotional reactivity. AI systems that provide instant, effortless outputs are reward-delivery systems for the brain — and habitual use may train the dopamine system to expect immediate resolution of cognitive tasks, lowering tolerance for the delay and effort that genuine thinking requires.

Mark (2023). Attention Span · Koessmeier & Büttner (2023). Scientific Reports · De et al. (2025). Cureus · Bo et al. (2024). Nature Neuroscience · Brevet-Aeby et al. (2016). Neuroscience & Biobehavioral Reviews

Working Memory

Working memory is the brain's active workspace — the system that holds and manipulates information in real time during thinking. Although working memory capacity has traditionally been viewed as a fixed trait, research shows it can be improved through adaptive training, with training associated with changes in frontal and parietal cortex activity and dopamine receptor density. Cognitive offloading directly undermines this plasticity: participants who offloaded more performed less accurately on subsequent memory tests, even when they knew a test was coming. The dorsolateral prefrontal cortex — the region most involved in working memory — is also the region most directly suppressed during passive AI-assisted task completion. Moderate cognitive demands engage prefrontal-parietal networks optimally, producing beneficial neurotransmitter release and supporting long-term potentiation. AI delegation consistently pushes tasks below the threshold of beneficial engagement.

One important nuance — and this matters for the ethics of the tool — is that participants with lower working memory capacity generally benefitted more from cognitive offloading, particularly under high memory load. AI assistance acts as a genuine equaliser for people whose working memory is already under strain: from anxiety, ADHD, high cognitive load, or neurodivergence. This simulation does not deny that benefit. It asks whether it should be the default for everyone.

Grinschgl et al. (2021). QJEP, 74(9) · Olesen et al. (2004). Nature Neuroscience, 7(1) · Klingberg (2010). Trends in Cognitive Sciences, 14(7) · Espeso-García (2025). Cultura, Ciencia y Deporte

About this game

What is vAIl of Ignorance? An interactive simulation that puts you inside four everyday cognitive tasks — writing, summarising, brainstorming, and explaining — and shows you in real time what happens to your brain when you do them yourself versus when you delegate them to AI.

How does the brain activation work? The brain regions shown are schematic representations grounded in published neuroscience research. They are not clinical measurements — they are educational approximations based on fMRI studies of language production, memory encoding, creativity, and executive function. The AI's activation is fixed at ~12% because reading output primarily engages the visual cortex and shallow language processing, without activating the hippocampal encoding or working memory networks that produce durable learning.

How is professionalism / creativity / relevance scored? Your text is scored in real time by Claude (Anthropic's AI) using a task-specific prompt that compares your output to the AI's reference output. The score reflects that specific dimension — not your writing quality overall.

The ethical dilemma

The core tension. AI delegation produces measurable short-term gains — faster output, more polished text, more ideas per minute. It also produces measurable costs — reduced hippocampal encoding, lower working memory exercise, atrophied self-efficacy. This tool does not tell you the costs outweigh the benefits. It asks you to see both clearly.

justice as fairness — Rawls

Imagine you are behind a veil of ignorance — you don't know whether you are someone who grew up before AI and had years to build cognitive capacities, or someone entering education now, forming habits before those capacities exist. From behind that veil, would you choose a world where cognitive autonomy depends on the accident of when you were born? Those who never develop the capacity to write, summarise, or generate ideas independently don't know what they've lost. That asymmetry is the justice problem.

utilitarianism

The utilitarian calculus depends entirely on timeframe and who you're measuring. In the short term, for the individual, AI delegation is a clear win. In the long term, across populations, the picture is murkier: homogenised creative output, reduced independent comprehension, and a growing dependence on tools whose access, reliability, and alignment cannot be guaranteed. Whether the aggregate benefit outweighs the aggregate cost is genuinely unresolved.

This tool was designed not to answer this question but to make it impossible to ignore.

The builder

vAIl of Ignorance was built as a prototype ethics tool for the CME Individual Assignment, CSSci, April 2026.

Research question: How do we decide what we let AI do for us? Specifically — what is the neurological cost of cognitive delegation, and is that cost distributed equitably across society?

Ethical frameworks applied: Justice as fairness (Rawls, 1971) as primary framework, with utilitarian analysis of short-term vs long-term costs and benefits.

Target audience: Students and knowledge workers aged 18–35 who regularly use AI tools — people for whom the ethical dilemma is live and immediate, not abstract.

Design intention: The tool does not lecture. It places the user inside the dilemma — making four decisions, seeing four consequences — and leaves them with a question they didn't have before they started.

Technical note: Brain activation values are schematic educational representations based on published fMRI and EEG research. Professionalism, creativity, contextual relevance, and depth of understanding scores are generated in real time by Claude (claude-sonnet-4-6) using task-specific scoring prompts.
